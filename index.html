Cuteness in relation to dragqueens

The peculiar relation between cute and dragqueens has to be explained. While it’s not true that all dragqueens are, there is no doubt a peculiar affinity and overlap. The cute insistence on not being ‘serious’, on playing, also connects with the dragqueens desire to remain youthfull.

To me a lot of dragqueens are surely cute, like Kim Chi and Trixie Mattel. But my sister thinks that dragqueens are ‘meer kattig dan schattig’. Which I can agree on as well, but I think that it’s because that a more of a caricature of cute and their can also be parts of a queen which are cute like the fact that they love to wear bright colours and loads of glitter. Or maybe it’s because dragqueens are always over the top, more that a ‘real’ woman. They have too much of everything. It feels a bit as if playing with dolls when I was little, and making up stories of how I would be when I grow up (Which never was to realistic). But then the dragqueens became a caricature of those dolls. That sometimes reminds me of Disney princesses that are perfectly polished en wear bright colourful glittery gowns just like dragqueens.

One of my favourite and therefore one of the cutest dragqueens is Kim Chi. She is very goofy and clumsy in her way of moving and talking which sometimes is a bit child like. Also her looks are always very imaginative, with a lot of bright and pastel colours. She always has really.

This cute innocent look can also be a mask, for example the Japanese artist Yoshitomo Nara paints a lot of young innocent looking girls with big heads (also a lot of dogs). Although the children and dogs he creates are adorable they are often ominous, causing viewers to contemplate the feelings and concepts behind his work. Underneath the popular appeal of the dark but adorable characters in his work are the somber social, political, and personal elements of his work: darker emotions of loneliness in a rigid society, rage, fear, and helplessness. Nara uses soft pastels and the apiece of cute and vulnerable children and animals as opposed to punk rock imagery. He also takes inspiration from the positive values of Japanese tradition and combines traditional with contemporary. The subjects of his work, the wide-eyed children and animals, whose vulnerability and in addition to the nightmarish features of his paintings can easily stimulate distressing feelings.
